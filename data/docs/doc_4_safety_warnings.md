# Heart Failure Warning Signs and Safety

> **Disclaimer:** This document is general health education, paraphrased from public
> patient resources. It is **not medical advice**. If you think you are having an emergency,
> call your local emergency number (such as 911) right away.

## Call emergency services (911) immediately for these signs

Some symptoms can signal a medical emergency such as a heart attack, stroke, or severe heart
failure, and need an immediate call to emergency services:

- **Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or discomfort** that does not go away
- **Severe or sudden shortness of breath**, or trouble breathing at rest
- **Fainting or passing out**, or feeling like you are about to pass out
- **Severe or sudden dizziness**
- **Signs of stroke**: sudden face drooping, arm weakness, or difficulty speaking
- **Coughing up pink, frothy mucus or blood**
- **Blue or gray lips or fingertips**

Do not drive yourself if you are having these symptoms. Call for emergency help.

## Call your clinician soon (not an emergency, but important) for these changes

These changes may mean your heart failure is getting worse and should be reported promptly:

- **Rapid weight gain**, such as 2–3 pounds in a day or about 5 pounds in a week
- **Increased swelling** in the legs, ankles, feet, or abdomen
- **Worsening shortness of breath** with your usual activities
- Needing **more pillows** than usual to sleep, or waking up breathless
- A persistent cough or wheezing, or a feeling of new fatigue

## Everyday medication safety

- **Do not stop or change your medicines** on your own without talking to your clinician.
- Be cautious with **over-the-counter NSAID pain relievers** (such as ibuprofen or naproxen),
  which can cause fluid retention; ask before using them.
- Keep an **up-to-date list of your medicines** and share it with every clinician you see.
- Check with your **pharmacist or clinician before starting any new medicine or supplement.**

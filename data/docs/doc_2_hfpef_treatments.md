# Treatment Options for HFpEF

> **Disclaimer:** This document is general health education, paraphrased from public
> patient resources. It is **not medical advice** and does **not** recommend any specific
> medication, dose, or treatment for you. Treatment decisions must be made with your clinician.

## General goals of treatment

The main goals in HFpEF are to **relieve symptoms, reduce fluid buildup, prevent
hospitalizations, and treat the conditions that contribute to it** (such as high blood
pressure, diabetes, obesity, kidney disease, and atrial fibrillation). Because HFpEF is
closely tied to these other conditions, managing them well is a central part of care.

## Medications that may be used

- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (for example, dapagliflozin or empagliflozin) are now recommended for
  many people with HFpEF. Studies show they can lower the risk of hospitalization for heart
  failure. They were first used for diabetes but also help the heart and kidneys.
- **Diuretics** ("water pills") help the body remove extra fluid, which can ease shortness of
  breath and swelling. They treat symptoms of congestion rather than the underlying stiffness.
- **Blood pressure medicines** are important because controlling blood pressure reduces strain
  on the heart. These may include several different drug classes depending on the person.
- **Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs)**, such as spironolactone, may be considered
  for some patients.
- Medicines to control **heart rate and rhythm** may be used if atrial fibrillation is present.

The right combination depends on your other health conditions, your kidney function, and how
you respond. Only your clinician can decide what is appropriate for you.

## Lifestyle and self-care that support treatment

- Following a **lower-sodium (salt) eating pattern** to reduce fluid retention
- **Monitoring your weight** daily and reporting rapid changes
- Staying **physically active** within limits your clinician approves
- **Managing weight, blood sugar, and blood pressure**
- Taking medicines exactly as prescribed and not stopping them on your own

## A note on over-the-counter medicines

Some common over-the-counter drugs, such as NSAID pain relievers (for example ibuprofen or
naproxen), can cause the body to hold on to fluid and may worsen heart failure for some
people. Ask your pharmacist or clinician before starting any new medicine or supplement.

# HFpEF: An Overview for Patients

> **Disclaimer:** This document is general health education, paraphrased from public
> patient resources. It is **not medical advice** and is **not specific to any individual**.
> Always talk with your own clinician about your situation.

## What is HFpEF?

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) is a type of heart failure in which
the heart's main pumping chamber (the left ventricle) squeezes normally but has become
**stiff and does not relax or fill with blood as easily as it should**. "Ejection fraction"
(EF) is the percentage of blood the left ventricle pumps out with each beat. In HFpEF the
ejection fraction is preserved, usually **50% or higher**, even though the heart is not
working normally. This is different from heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
(HFrEF), where the ejection fraction is low (often below 40%).

Because the stiff heart cannot fill properly, pressure can build up and fluid can back up
into the lungs and the rest of the body. "Heart failure" does **not** mean the heart has
stopped; it means the heart is not keeping up with the body's needs as well as it should.

## Common symptoms

People with HFpEF often notice:

- Shortness of breath, especially with activity or when lying flat
- Fatigue and feeling tired or weak
- Swelling (edema) in the legs, ankles, feet, or abdomen
- Rapid weight gain from fluid buildup
- Reduced ability to exercise or do usual activities
- Needing extra pillows to sleep, or waking up short of breath

## Who is more likely to develop HFpEF?

HFpEF becomes more common with age and is frequently linked with other conditions,
including **high blood pressure, obesity, type 2 diabetes, kidney disease, and atrial
fibrillation**. It is diagnosed more often in women and in older adults. Long-standing high
blood pressure is one of the most important contributors because it makes the heart muscle
thicken and stiffen over time.
